Budget & Financial

Allowable Cost
An expense that is permitted by the grant and necessary to complete the project's goals. Examples include curriculum materials, staff stipends for grant-related activities, and travel for project implementation. Costs must be reasonable, allocable, and documented.
Unallowable Cost
Any expense explicitly prohibited by the grant’s rules. Common examples include entertainment, personal gifts, and any cost unrelated to the project’s goals. Always confirm questionable costs with the Grants Office to ensure compliance.
Budget
A detailed estimate of expenses expected during the grant period. Budgets break down costs line-by-line, such as $2,500 for instructional supplies or $1,200 for after-school snacks for participants.
Budget Narrative
A written justification for each item in your budget. This explains how each cost supports the project, helping reviewers understand why the funds are needed and appropriate.
Indirect Cost
Expenses that support the project but aren't tied to a specific activity, such as utilities or administrative support. Most grants limit the amount of indirect costs based on a negotiated rate.
Match
A required financial or in-kind contribution by the district or partners. This may include cash from department funds, staff time, donated space, or services provided by collaborators.

Project Planning & Design

Fidelity of Implementation
Delivering the project as originally proposed—on time, with planned activities, and using approved materials. Maintaining fidelity shows accountability and ensures the program’s effectiveness can be evaluated properly.
Logic Model
A visual or written representation that outlines the project's structure: Inputs (resources), Activities, Outputs (deliverables), Outcomes (short-term effects), and Impact (long-term change). A strong logic model connects activities to measurable results.
Project-Based Approach
Designing grant proposals around clear, time-bound, goal-oriented efforts. Instead of describing daily duties, this means framing ideas as specific initiatives with objectives and measurable outcomes—for example, a semester-long afterschool robotics program.
Sustainability Plan
A description of how the project will continue after grant funding ends. This may include training staff, integrating activities into regular budgets, or forming long-term partnerships.
Timeline
A schedule showing when major project activities will occur, including start/end dates, milestones, and deadlines for deliverables and reports.

Compliance & Reporting

Monitoring
The process of reviewing a grant project to ensure it complies with all legal, financial, and programmatic requirements. Can be conducted by funders, the district, or independent auditors.
Amendment
A formal request to change part of your approved grant—such as adjusting the budget or timeline. Most funders require prior approval for significant changes.
Performance Report
A required update (quarterly or annually) submitted to the funder describing progress on project goals, how funds were spent, and outcomes achieved.
Closeout
The final step in the grant lifecycle, involving final financial reconciliation, submission of all reports, and confirmation that all grant requirements were met.

General Grant Terms

RFP (Request for Proposals)
A document issued by a funding agency that explains the purpose of a grant, eligibility criteria, allowable uses of funds, application instructions, evaluation criteria, and deadlines.
NOFO (Notice of Funding Opportunity)
Another term for RFP, most commonly used by federal agencies. It provides a comprehensive overview of a grant opportunity.
MOU (Memorandum of Understanding)
A formal agreement between the district and a partner organization outlining roles, responsibilities, and commitments for the grant project.
Evidence-Based Practice
A strategy, activity, or intervention supported by rigorous research showing positive outcomes. Many grants require applicants to use or reference evidence-based approaches.
Stakeholder Engagement
The involvement of key individuals (e.g., families, students, community organizations, administrators) in planning, executing, or evaluating the project. Some grants require documented outreach to stakeholders.
